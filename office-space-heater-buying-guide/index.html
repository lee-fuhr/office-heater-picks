How to choose the right office space heater

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

For an office space heater in 2026, prioritize safety features like tip-over and overheat shutoff, a quiet enough noise level for your workspace, and a size that matches your desk area and heating needs without overloading circuits. Look for models with adjustable thermostats and timers to avoid wasting energy, and choose a type, convection or radiant, based on whether you want whole-room warmth or focused personal heat.

What safety features should I look for in an office space heater?

Safety is the top concern when using a space heater in an office, where you may be distracted or away from the unit. Essential features include automatic tip-over shutoff (the heater turns off if it is knocked over) and overheat protection (it shuts off if internal temperatures get dangerously high). Many modern heaters also have cool-touch exteriors, which is a real benefit if you work in a tight space and might brush against the unit. Look for a heater certified by a recognized testing laboratory such as UL or ETL. This certification indicates the unit has passed rigorous safety tests. Avoid using heaters with frayed cords or that feel hot to the touch on the outside, even if they have safety claims. Also, be aware of the heater’s placement: keep it at least three feet away from papers, curtains, and other flammable materials. A heater with a built-in tip-over switch gives you one less thing to worry about during a busy workday.

How quiet should my office space heater be?

Noise level can make or break a space heater for office use. A heater that rumbles, clicks, or hums loudly can be a major distraction during phone calls, meetings, or focused work. Most manufacturers list noise ratings in decibels, but general buyer feedback suggests that heaters around 40 to 50 dB are acceptable for open offices, while anything above 55 dB may be intrusive in a quiet environment. Oil-filled radiator heaters are often the quietest, producing little more than a soft tick as the oil warms. Ceramic fan heaters tend to be noisier due to the fan, but many have a “whisper” mode that cuts noise noticeably. If you work in a shared space, consider a heater with a low-noise setting or a convection heater that has no fan at all. It is always wise to read owner reviews that specifically mention noise before purchasing.

How do I choose the right size and power for my office?

Heater power is typically measured in watts, and most portable space heaters in North America top out at 1,500 watts to avoid overloading a standard 15-amp circuit. For a typical small office or cubicle (around 100 to 150 square feet), a 750 to 1,500 watt heater should be sufficient. Bigger rooms or drafty spaces will need the higher end of that range. Size also refers to physical footprint. A tall, slim tower heater may tuck neatly beside a desk, while a low, wide model might take up too much floor space. Before buying, measure the spot you plan to place it and account for the required clearance around the heater. Also, check your office’s electrical setup: avoid daisy-chaining heaters with other high-power devices on the same circuit, especially if the heater has a constant high setting.

What is the best type of heater for an office: convection or radiant?

Convection heaters warm the air gradually and circulate it around the room, making them a better choice for whole-office comfort over several hours. Examples include oil-filled radiators, baseboard heaters, and ceramic fan heaters. They take longer to heat up but maintain an even temperature, which is ideal if you plan to stay in one spot for a while. Radiant heaters, like quartz or infrared models, heat objects and people directly without warming the air much. They are great for spot heating, point one at your feet from under the desk and you will feel warmth almost instantly. However, they can leave the rest of the room cold and may not be as comfortable for long stretches. For most office setups, a quiet convection heater (especially an oil-filled radiator) offers the best balance of comfort, safety, and low noise.

How can I save energy with an office space heater?

Space heaters are inherently energy-hungry, so using them wisely matters. Look for a model with an adjustable thermostat and a timer. A thermostat prevents the heater from running constantly, cycling on and off to maintain the set temperature. Some heaters also have an eco mode that automatically lowers power when the room is warm enough. Use the heater to supplement your office’s existing heating, not replace it entirely. Set the heater to keep your immediate area comfortable rather than heating the whole room. A programmable timer can turn the heater off after lunch or at the end of the workday, many modern units include this. Also, consider using a heater with multiple heat settings (high/low) so you can dial back power when the weather is mild. And never leave a heater unattended for long periods; turning it off when you step away can save energy and reduce risk.

What controls and smart features are actually useful?

Not all bells and whistles are worth the extra cost. A basic digital thermostat with a clear display that shows the current room temperature and your set point is genuinely helpful. A remote control can be convenient, especially if the heater is placed under your desk where the controls are hard to reach. Smart features like Wi-Fi connectivity, smartphone apps, and voice assistant compatibility are becoming common. These allow you to schedule the heater, turn it on before you arrive, or adjust it without leaving your seat. However, they often add to the price and complexity. For a typical office, a simple unit with a thermostat, a timer, and a few heat settings will serve you well. Just make sure the controls are intuitive and easy to read, fumbling with tiny buttons in a cold morning office is frustrating.

Frequently asked questions

Are space heaters safe to leave on overnight?

Most portable space heaters are not designed for unattended or overnight use, especially in an office that may be empty. Even with safety certifications, it is best to turn the heater off when you leave for the day or go home. If you need heat after hours, check if your building has a policy about space heaters.

Will a space heater trip my circuit breaker?

It can happen, especially if the heater is on the same circuit as other power-hungry devices like computers, monitors, or printers. A standard 1,500-watt heater draws about 12.5 amps, which is close to the 15-amp limit of many circuits. To avoid trips, plug the heater directly into a wall outlet (never an extension cord or power strip) and avoid running other high-wattage equipment on the same circuit.

How much does it cost to run an office space heater?

Running a 1,500-watt heater for eight hours a day at average electricity rates can cost somewhere in the range of a few dollars per day. Exact costs depend on your local electricity price and how many hours you run the heater. Using a thermostat and lower power settings can reduce that cost noticeably.

Do space heaters produce carbon monoxide?

Electric space heaters do not produce carbon monoxide because they don’t burn fuel. Only combustion-based heaters (kerosene, propane, or natural gas) can produce CO. If you use an electric heater in an office, you are safe from carbon monoxide risks.

Can I use a space heater with a power strip?

No. Space heaters should always be plugged directly into a wall outlet. Power strips and extension cords can overheat and cause a fire, even if the strip is rated for the wattage. This is a common safety warning in every heater manual.

What is the most energy-efficient type of space heater?

All electric space heaters convert nearly 100% of the electricity into heat, so in theory they are equally efficient. In practice, the most efficient type is one that matches your use case: a convection heater with a thermostat for long-term use, or a radiant heater for spot heating. Smart features and timers help you avoid waste, but no type is inherently far more efficient than another.

In shortFinding the right office space heater is about balancing safety, noise, power, and features for your specific workspace. Focus on a certified model with essential safety shutoffs, a noise level you can work alongside, and a size that suits your desk area and electrical setup. Pair that with smart usage habits, using a thermostat, avoiding power strips, and turning it off when not needed, and you will stay comfortably warm without blowing your budget or your circuits.
